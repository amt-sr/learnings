Service Level Objective (SLO)

Difficulty ⭐⭐⭐ Interview Frequency ★★★
Goal: Understand SLOs as internal reliability targets backed by SLI measurements, the concept of error budgets, and how SLOs balance reliability with velocity.

Definition

A Service Level Objective (SLO) is an internal target for a service's reliability, expressed as a measurable goal over a time window. Unlike SLAs (external contracts), SLOs are engineering targets that teams use to make tradeoff decisions. SLOs are defined in terms of SLIs (Service Level Indicators) — the actual measurements.

SLO Anatomy

Every well-formed SLO has these components:

SLO = { SLI: "availability of the /api/orders endpoint", target: "99.95%", time_window: "rolling 30 days", scope: "excluding admin operations and batch jobs", alerting: "page on-call if 2% of 30-day budget consumed in 1 hour" }

Error Budget

The error budget is the inverse of the SLO target. It defines how much unreliability is "allowed" within the time window. This is the key concept that makes SLOs actionable.

Core Insight: Error budgets are what let teams balance reliability work against feature work. When the budget is nearly exhausted, the team stops shipping features and focuses on reliability. When the budget is healthy, the team can move fast.

Error Budget Calculation

// 30-day window, 99.9% SLO target
Total minutes in 30 days = 30 × 24 × 60 = 43,200 minutes
Allowed downtime         = 43,200 × (1 - 0.999) = 43.2 minutes

// If current consumed budget is 20 minutes:
//  Remaining budget = 43.2 - 20 = 23.2 minutes
//  Budget health     = 20 / 43.2 = 46% consumed

Error Budget Policy

Budget RemainingAction
> 50%Normal feature velocity; experiments and aggressive deploys are fine
25-50%Slow down risky releases; prioritize reliability bug fixes
< 25%Feature freeze; all hands on reliability; canary-first deployments only
ExhaustedStop all non-essential changes; page on-call for any degradation

SLO vs SLA Relationship

SLA: 99.9% (contract with customer) │ │ Safety margin ▼ SLO: 99.95% (internal engineering target) │ │ Alerting threshold ▼ Alert: 99.92% (page on-call) │ │ Current measurement ▼ SLI: 99.96% (actual observed value)
Why SLO > SLA? Your internal target should be stricter than your external commitment. If your SLO equals your SLA, you will breach the SLA before you have time to react. A 0.05% gap gives the team time to respond before a breach.

Choosing the Right SLO Target

Higher SLOs are not always better. The cost curve is exponential past 99.9%.

Service CategoryTypical SLORationale
Payment processing99.99%+Direct revenue impact; every error costs money
Core API / Authentication99.95%Cascading failure risk; blocks all dependent services
Internal dashboard99.5%Non-critical; engineers can tolerate brief outages
Analytics pipeline99.0%Batch processing; delayed results are acceptable
Recommendations / ML serving99.5%Fallback to defaults is acceptable

Multi-SLO Services

Real services have multiple SLOs that must be tracked independently:

Service: "Order Service" ├── SLO 1: Availability ≥ 99.95% (error budget: 21.9 min/month) ├── SLO 2: p99 latency ≤ 200ms (error budget: 5% of requests) └── SLO 3: Correctness ≥ 99.999% (error budget: 4.3 min/month) ↑ Correctness SLO is much tighter because data corruption is far worse than brief unavailability

Common Interview Questions

  • "What is an error budget and how do you use it?" — Define remaining unreliability; use it to gate feature releases vs reliability work.
  • "How do you decide on an SLO target?" — Business impact analysis: cost of downtime vs cost of achieving higher reliability.
  • "What happens when you exhaust your error budget?" — Feature freeze, all-hands on reliability, canary-only deploys.
  • "How do SLOs and SLAs relate?" — SLO is the internal target, SLA is the external contract. SLO should be stricter than SLA.
  • "How would you set SLOs for a new microservice?" — Start conservative (99%), measure SLIs for 2-4 weeks, then tighten based on data.
  • "What if your dependency's SLO is worse than yours?" — Graceful degradation, caching, async fallbacks, or renegotiate the dependency SLO.
