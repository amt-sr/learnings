Recovery Time Objective (RTO)

Difficulty вӯҗвӯҗвӯҗ Interview Frequency вҳ…вҳ…вҳ…вҳ…
Goal: Understand RTO as a business-driven metric for acceptable downtime, how it drives failover architecture and automation decisions, and the four standard DR strategies (backup/cold/warm/hot standby).

Definition

Recovery Time Objective (RTO) is the maximum acceptable duration of service downtime after a failure event. It defines how quickly a system must be restored to an operational state. If RTO = 30 minutes, the system must be fully functional within 30 minutes of a disaster. RTO includes the entire recovery process: detection, provisioning, restoring data, replaying logs, verification, and traffic cutover.

RTO answers the question: "How long can we afford to be down?" вҖ” Not "How much data can we lose?" (that is RPO). RTO is about time to restore service.

The Four DR Strategies

RTO drives the choice of disaster recovery strategy. The four standard tiers, from longest to shortest RTO:

1. Backup and Restore (RTO: hours to days)

Strategy: Periodic backups to off-site storage When disaster strikes: 1. Procure new infrastructure (hours) 2. Restore from last backup (hours) 3. Rebuild application stack (hours) Cost: вҳ…вҳҶвҳҶвҳҶвҳҶ (Lowest) RTO: hours to days RPO: hours (last backup) Automation: Low (mostly manual) Best for: Development environments, non-critical analytics

2. Cold Standby (RTO: 1-4 hours)

Strategy: Infrastructure provisioned but not running When disaster strikes: 1. Start pre-provisioned instances (10-30 min) 2. Restore data from backup (30-120 min) 3. Deploy application, configure (30-60 min) Cost: вҳ…вҳ…вҳҶвҳҶвҳҶ (Low) RTO: 1-4 hours RPO: hours (last backup) Automation: Medium (IaC for provisioning) Best for: Internal tools, non-revenue-critical services

3. Warm Standby (RTO: minutes to 1 hour)

Strategy: Infrastructure running with minimal capacity, data replicated asynchronously When disaster strikes: 1. Scale up warm standby (5-10 min) 2. Catch up replication lag (minutes) 3. Verify and cutover traffic (5-10 min) Cost: вҳ…вҳ…вҳ…вҳҶвҳҶ (Medium) RTO: 10-60 minutes RPO: seconds to minutes (async replication lag) Automation: High (auto-scaling, DNS failover) Best for: Most production services (typical choice for FAANG)

4. Hot Standby / Active-Active (RTO: seconds to minutes)

Strategy: Full capacity running in multiple regions, data replicated synchronously or semi-sync When disaster strikes: 1. Health check detects failure (seconds) 2. DNS / load balancer redirects (seconds) 3. Second region already serving at full capacity Cost: вҳ…вҳ…вҳ…вҳ…вҳ… (Highest вҖ” 2x+ infrastructure) RTO: seconds to ~1 minute RPO: 0 to seconds (sync replication) Automation: Full (automated failover, no human intervention) Best for: Payment, auth, and mission-critical revenue services

Cost of Downtime Calculation

RTO should be derived from the cost of downtime, not chosen arbitrarily:

Failover Automation

Key Insight: The difference between a 5-minute RTO and a 30-minute RTO is usually not infrastructure вҖ” it is automation. Manual failover involves: detecting the failure, making the decision, running runbooks, provisioning resources. Automated failover eliminates human latency.

Common Interview Questions

  • "What is RTO?" вҖ” The maximum acceptable downtime after a failure. How fast you must recover service.
  • "What is the difference between RPO and RTO?" вҖ” RPO is data loss tolerance (backward in time). RTO is downtime tolerance (forward in time). They are independent requirements.
  • "What are the four DR strategies?" вҖ” Backup/restore (hours), cold standby (1-4 hours), warm standby (10-60 min), hot standby/active-active (seconds). Trade off cost vs RTO.
  • "How would you design for an RTO of 5 minutes?" вҖ” Warm standby with automated failover: health monitoring triggers auto-scaling of standby instances, DNS failover, and smoke tests. Data replicated asynchronously with minimal lag.
  • "What is the difference between warm and hot standby?" вҖ” Warm: instances running but not at full capacity, need to scale up. Hot: full capacity running in multiple regions, failover is instant (DNS swap only).
  • "How do you calculate downtime cost?" вҖ” Revenue per hour Г— fraction affected + brand damage + compliance risk. Compare to DR infrastructure cost to justify the investment.
  • "What drives the decision between warm and hot standby?" вҖ” RTO requirements and cost tolerance. Hot standby costs 2-5x more but achieves seconds-level RTO. Warm standby is sufficient for most services (10-30 min RTO).
