Service Level Agreement (SLA)

Difficulty ⭐⭐ Interview Frequency ★★★★
Goal: Understand what an SLA is, how uptime percentages translate to real downtime, why SLAs matter in system design, and how they drive architectural decisions around redundancy and failover.

Definition

A Service Level Agreement (SLA) is a formal, legally binding contract between a service provider and a customer that defines the minimum expected level of service. It specifies measurable metrics (typically uptime), the consequences of failing to meet those targets (credits, penalties, or termination rights), and the scope of what is covered. SLAs are externally facing commitments that set customer expectations.

Understanding the math behind "nine" is critical for interviews. Every additional nine is an order of magnitude harder to achieve.

SLA vs SLO vs SLI

Key Distinction: SLAs are external, contractual commitments. SLOs are internal engineering targets. SLIs are the measurements. SLA targets are typically set below SLO targets to leave a safety margin.
ConceptAudienceNatureExample
SLACustomer / LegalContractual obligation"99.95% uptime or 10% credit"
SLOEngineering teamInternal target"99.99% uptime target"
SLIMonitoring systemMeasurement"Success rate of API calls = 99.97%"

Architectural Implications of SLA Targets

Higher SLA targets require progressively more expensive and complex architectures:

TargetRequired ArchitectureCost Multiplier
99.9%Multi-AZ within one region, active-passive1x
99.99%Multi-region active-passive with automated failover2-3x
99.999%Multi-region active-active, global DNS failover < 30s5-10x
99.9999%Global active-active with local failover, N+2 redundancy10-20x

Composite SLAs

In practice, end-to-end availability is the product of all component availabilities. If your app depends on three services each with 99.9% uptime:

End-to-end = 0.999 × 0.999 × 0.999 = 0.997 → 99.7% (25 hours downtime/year)

This is why senior engineers push for higher SLOs on dependencies or design for graceful degradation so one dependency cannot take down the whole system.

Planned Maintenance Exclusions

Most SLAs exclude planned maintenance windows. A typical clause:

Example Clause: "Scheduled maintenance occurring during the maintenance window of Sunday 00:00-04:00 UTC shall not count toward downtime calculations. Provider will give 72 hours advance notice."

Common Interview Questions

  • "How many 'nines' of availability does your system need?" — Think about the business impact per minute of downtime.
  • "What is the difference between SLA, SLO, and SLI?" — External contract vs internal target vs measurement.
  • "How would you design a system for 99.99% availability?" — Multi-region, auto-failover, circuit breakers, async writes.
  • "What happens when you chain services with 99.9% SLA each?" — Composite SLA multiplication degrades overall availability.
  • "How do you handle planned maintenance without SLA violations?" — Rolling deployments, blue-green, maintenance windows.
  • "How would you negotiate an SLA with a third-party vendor?" — Define measurement method, exclusions, credit tiers, and termination rights.

What to Skip

Depth: Legal contract negotiation tactics, specific vendor SLA terms (e.g., AWS S3 vs Azure Blob comparison).
Depth: Detailed penalty calculation formulas unless the interviewer asks.
Depth: ITIL/ITSM frameworks for SLA management — too process-heavy for system design interviews.
