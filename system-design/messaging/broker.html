8.2.1 Broker (Message Broker)

Difficulty: ⭐⭐☆☆☆ Interview Frequency: ★★★☆☆

Goal

Understand the role of a message broker as the middleware that sits between producers and consumers, managing message routing, persistence, and delivery guarantees.

Definition

A message broker is an intermediary service that receives messages from producers, routes them to the correct destination (queue or topic), and ensures reliable delivery to consumers. It abstracts away the complexity of direct producer-to-consumer communication, handling message storage, ordering, retry logic, and failure recovery.

Analogy: A message broker is like a post office. The sender (producer) drops off a letter without needing to know who will pick it up or whether the recipient is home. The post office (broker) stores, routes, and ensures delivery.

What the Broker Manages

Message Routing

The broker determines where each message should go. In simple queue-based systems, this is direct. In pub/sub systems, the broker fans out messages to all subscribers of a topic. Advanced brokers support content-based routing, header-based routing, and complex topology.

Persistence

Messages can be stored in memory (fast but lost on crash) or on disk (durable but slower). Most production brokers write to disk for durability. Kafka writes messages to disk as part of its append-only log, which is why it handles high throughput well.

Delivery Guarantees

The broker coordinates delivery semantics:

  • At most once: Message may be lost but never duplicated. Best-effort delivery.
  • At least once: Message is never lost but may be delivered multiple times. Requires idempotent consumers.
  • Exactly once: Message is delivered exactly once. Most complex guarantee, offered by Kafka with idempotent producer + transactions.

Flow Control & Backpressure

The broker manages the rate mismatch between fast producers and slow consumers. It buffers messages so producers are not blocked. However, if the broker itself runs out of capacity, it must apply backpressure to producers.

When to Use a Broker in System Design

Rule of thumb: Introduce a message broker when you need to decouple services, handle asynchronous processing, buffer traffic spikes, or broadcast events to multiple consumers. If your system is small and synchronous, a broker adds unnecessary complexity.

Common Interview Questions

  1. "Compare Kafka, RabbitMQ, and SQS — when would you use each?"
  2. "What role does the broker play in a microservices architecture?"
  3. "Why not just use HTTP calls between services instead of a broker?"
  4. "How does a broker handle persistence and durability?"
  5. "What happens when the broker itself fails?"
  6. "Design a system that uses both Kafka and RabbitMQ — why would you need both?"
  7. "How does backpressure work in a broker-based system?"

What to Skip

Do not dive into:
  • Broker internals like Kafka's storage layout, index files, or ZooKeeper/KRaft internals — those are too deep for most interviews.
  • RabbitMQ exchange types in exhaustive detail unless specifically asked about routing.
  • Broker benchmark numbers (exact throughput/latency figures) — trends matter more than numbers.
