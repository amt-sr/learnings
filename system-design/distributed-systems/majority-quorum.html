Majority Quorum

Difficulty: ⭐⭐⭐☆☆ Interview Frequency: ★★★★★

Goal

Understand:

  • What majority quorum is and why it's the most common quorum configuration
  • How majority quorum relates to consensus algorithms (Raft, Paxos)
  • Why majority ensures safety (at most one conflicting decision)
  • Tolerance to node failures at different replication factors

Definition

Majority quorum requires more than half of the nodes to agree (strictly greater than N/2). With majority quorum for both reads and writes, the system guarantees strong consistency while tolerating up to floor(N/2) - 1 node failures.

Majority Quorum: Q = floor(N/2) + 1 N=3: majority = 2 → tolerates 1 failure N=5: majority = 3 → tolerates 2 failures N=7: majority = 4 → tolerates 3 failures N=9: majority = 5 → tolerates 4 failures Why majority guarantees no two conflicting decisions: With N=5, majority = 3: Group A decides X (3 nodes: A, B, C) Group B decides Y (3 nodes: D, E, ?) But there are only 5 nodes! A+B+C+D+E = 5 A,B,C need 3 votes → only 2 left D,E can only get 2 votes → NOT a majority → Two majorities must overlap in at least one node → That overlapping node won't vote for two conflicting decisions → At most one decision can achieve majority

Odd vs even N: Always use an odd number of replicas. N=4 has majority 2 (tolerates 1 failure) — same as N=3 but with more overhead. N=5 has majority 3 (tolerates 2 failures) — strictly better than N=4. In practice, use N=3 or N=5.

Majority in Consensus Algorithms

Majority quorum is the core safety mechanism in both Raft and Paxos:

Raft: Leader needs majority for both election and log replication Election (5 nodes): Candidate gets votes: A ✓, B ✓, C ✓ → 3 = majority → becomes leader D and E may have voted for someone else or not voted yet Log replication: Leader appends entry, sends to all followers Waits for majority to acknowledge → commits Followers that missed can catch up later Safety: Two leaders can't exist in same term (Each term can have at most one majority winner) Paxos: Same majority principle Phase 1 (Prepare): proposer needs majority promise Phase 2 (Accept): proposer needs majority accept Same safety argument: overlapping majority prevents conflicts

Common Interview Questions

  • "What is a majority quorum?"
    Requires strictly more than half of nodes (floor(N/2)+1) to agree. It's the most common quorum type because it guarantees safety (no conflicting decisions) while tolerating floor(N/2)-1 failures. Used in Raft, Paxos, ZooKeeper, etcd, and Dynamo-style systems.
  • "Why should N be odd?"
    With even N, you get the same fault tolerance as N-1 but with more overhead. N=4 (majority=2, tolerates 1 failure) is strictly worse than N=3 (majority=2, tolerates 1 failure) because N=4 costs an extra node for the same guarantee. Always prefer odd N (3, 5, or 7).
  • "How many node failures can a 5-node cluster with majority quorum tolerate?"
    Majority of 5 is 3. The system works as long as at least 3 nodes are available. So it tolerates 2 failures. If a 3rd node fails, only 2 remain — not a majority — and the system stops accepting writes (becomes unavailable).
  • "How does majority quorum relate to Raft?"
    Raft uses majority quorum for both leader election and log replication. A candidate needs votes from a majority to become leader. A leader needs acknowledgments from a majority to commit a log entry. The overlapping majority property guarantees that at most one leader per term and that committed entries are never lost.

What to Skip

  • Weighted quorum systems (voting with different weights)
  • Byzantine fault tolerance quorums (BFT requires 2/3+1, different context)
  • Grid quorums and hierarchical quorums (academic)
