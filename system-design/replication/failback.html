Failback

Difficulty: ⭐⭐⭐☆☆ Interview Frequency: ★★★☆☆

Goal

Understand the failback process — reverting to the original leader after a failover — and why many modern systems choose to avoid it entirely.

Definition

Failback is the process of returning the leadership role to the original leader node after a failover event. When the old leader recovers and comes back online, failback restores it to the leader position and demotes the temporary leader back to a follower.

Why Failback Is Hard

ORIGINAL STATE: [Leader A] --replicate--> [Follower B] --replicate--> [Follower C] FAILOVER (A crashes): [Leader A: DOWN] [Follower B -> PROMOTED] [Follower C] | | B accepts writes: w5, w6, w7, w8 | B replicates to C | B's log: [w1, w2, w3, w4, w5, w6, w7, w8] | C's log: [w1, w2, w3, w4, w5, w6, w7, w8] | A RECOVERS: [Leader A: BACK ONLINE] [Leader B: still leader] [Follower C] A's log: [w1, w2, w3, w4] B's log: [w1..w8] C's log: [w1..w8] PROBLEM: A is MISSING w5..w8 A also may have committed w4.5 that B never saw (if async) | v FAILOVER TO A (FAILBACK): Step 1: Sync A from B (A needs w5..w8) Step 2: Resolve any conflicts (w4.5 on A that B doesn't have) Step 3: Promote A to leader Step 4: Demote B back to follower Step 5: Redirect all traffic back to A Step 6: B now replicates from A (which it was leading moments ago) | RISKS: Data loss during switchover, brief unavailability, application connection churn, potential split brain if timing is off.

Failback Process Steps

  1. Old leader rejoins as follower: The recovered old leader syncs from the current (temporary) leader, applying all writes it missed during the outage.
  2. Conflict resolution: If the old leader had committed writes that the temporary leader never received (async replication gap), those writes must be reconciled. This may require manual intervention.
  3. Cooldown period: Wait for the old leader to fully catch up and stabilize. Verify replication lag is zero.
  4. Planned switchover: Perform a controlled failover from the temporary leader back to the original leader. This is essentially a planned failover.
  5. Traffic rerouting: Update DNS, VIP, or proxy to point back to the original leader. Clients reconnect.

Risks of Failback

Risk Description Severity
Data loss during switchover The switchover from B back to A is itself a failover, with all the associated risks (stale data, split brain, connection drops). High
Double failover instability Two leadership changes in quick succession increase the window for things to go wrong. Each failover carries risk. High
Connection churn Applications must reconnect twice: once when the original leader fails, once during failback. Medium
Hidden conflicts The old leader may have unreplicated writes that surface only after failback reconciliation. High

The Modern Approach: Avoid Failback

Most modern systems do not failback. Instead, they permanently promote the new leader and let the old leader rejoin as a follower. This avoids the complexity and risk of a second switchover.

MODERN APPROACH (No Failback): [Leader A] --replicate--> [Follower B] --replicate--> [Follower C] FAILOVER (A crashes): [Leader A: DOWN] [Leader B] (permanent) [Follower C] | B stays leader permanently A recovers and joins as follower | | v v [Leader B] --replicate--> [Follower A] --replicate--> [Follower C] BENEFITS: - Only one failover event (less risk) - A simply syncs from B (standard replication) - No conflicts (A has no writes that B doesn't have, since A was down) - Simpler orchestration - No traffic rerouting back
Industry standard: PostgreSQL Patroni, Orchestrator for MySQL, and Raft-based systems (etcd, CockroachDB) all promote the new leader permanently. The old leader rejoins as a follower. Failback is only used when the original leader had specific hardware advantages (faster disk, more RAM) and the operator wants to rebalance.

When Failback Makes Sense

There are a few scenarios where intentional failback is appropriate:

  • Hardware rebalancing: The original leader runs on more powerful hardware, and the temporary leader is on a weaker node. Failback optimizes for performance.
  • Geographic rebalancing: The temporary leader is in a different data center with higher latency to clients. Failback to the preferred DC.
  • Planned maintenance: A planned switchover (not an emergency failover) where the operator controls the timing and can verify zero replication lag before switching.
  • Symmetric cluster design: In some traditional database setups, the "primary" role is tied to a specific node (e.g., a specific IP address or host), and operators want to restore the original topology.

Common Interview Questions

  1. "After a failover, do you failback to the original leader?" — Generally no. Permanently promote the new leader. Failback adds a second failover's worth of risk. Only failback for planned rebalancing, not as a standard procedure.
  2. "What problems does failback introduce?" — Double failover risk, connection churn, conflict resolution for writes only the old leader had, potential split brain during the second switchover.
  3. "How would you implement safe failback if required?" — (1) Old leader rejoins as follower and fully syncs. (2) Verify zero lag via checksums. (3) Perform a planned, controlled switchover (essentially the same as a planned failover). (4) Verify all followers are healthy before cutting over.
  4. "How does Patroni handle failback?" &mdash> Patroni does not failback by default. When the old leader recovers, it joins as a follower. If the operator wants to move leadership back, they trigger a controlled switchover via patronictl switchover.

What to Skip

Avoid DR failback specifics: Disaster recovery failback (replicating from a warm standby site back to the primary site after an outage) is a different topic from database leader failback. Do not conflate the two.

Avoid DNS TTL manipulation: While DNS TTL is relevant to traffic rerouting, the specific DNS configuration is an infrastructure concern, not a system design concept.

Aavoid failback in stateful services: StatefulSet failback in Kubernetes is a niche topic. Focus on database failback.
