Failover

Difficulty: ⭐⭐⭐⭐☆ Interview Frequency: ★★★★★

Goal

Understand the failover process in replicated systems — how a follower is promoted to leader when the primary fails, the challenges involved, and the techniques that make failover reliable and fast.

Definition

Failover is the process of switching from a failed or unhealthy leader (primary) to a standby replica (follower) so that the system can continue accepting writes. The promoted replica takes over the leader role, and clients are re-routed to it.

Failover Process

Step 1: FAULT DETECTION ========================= Monitor detects Leader is unhealthy (heartbeat timeout, health check failure, unresponsive) Step 2: LEADER DEMOTION ====================== Old Leader marked as DOWN (or fenced out via lease/token) Step 3: FOLLOWER SELECTION ========================== Select best follower to promote Criteria: - Most up-to-date (least replication lag) - On same AZ as old leader (if possible) - Healthy disk, memory, CPU - In majority partition (quorum) Step 4: PROMOTION ================= New Leader: - Stop replication thread - Apply any remaining WAL - Accept writes - Notify other followers to replicate from it Step 5: CLIENT REROUTING ======================= DNS, VIP, or proxy update: - Point write traffic to new Leader - Update read pool (new Leader + remaining Followers) - Client retry on connection reset Step 6: RECONCILIATION ===================== Old Leader (if it recovers): - Detected as stale (has old writes) - Re-synced as Follower of new Leader - Or wiped and re-seeded

Manual vs. Automatic Failover

Aspect Manual Failover Automatic Failover
Trigger Human operator decides System detects failure and promotes
Speed Minutes to hours (human decision time) Seconds (detection + promotion)
False positive risk Low (human validates) High (brief glitch may trigger)
Data loss risk Can verify follower is caught up May promote lagging follower, losing recent writes
Operator load High (requires on-call response) Low (self-healing)
Common in Semi-sync systems, small teams Cloud-managed databases, large-scale systems

Problems with Failover

1. Stale Follower Promoted (Data Loss)

If the promoted follower is behind the leader, any writes that the leader committed but the follower did not receive are permanently lost. This is the Recovery Point Objective (RPO) gap.

Worst case: Leader commits 500 writes in the 10 seconds before crashing. Follower was 10 seconds behind. All 500 writes are lost. If those were financial transactions, this is catastrophic.

Mitigations:

  • Semi-synchronous replication: Leader waits for at least one follower to acknowledge receipt before committing. Guarantees at most one follower is fully up-to-date at all times.
  • Synchronous replication: Leader waits for all followers before committing. Zero data loss but high write latency.
  • Promote least-lagging follower: Always select the follower closest to the leader's position.

2. Split Brain During Failover

If the old leader is not truly dead (e.g., network partition) and the failover system promotes a new leader, both leaders may accept writes. See 4.6.4 Split Brain for details.

Mitigations:

  • Fencing tokens: Old leader's writes are rejected by storage if its token is stale.
  • Lease expiry: Old leader's lease expires before new leader is elected.
  • STONITH: Power off or network-isolate the old leader before promotion.

3. Failover Duration (Downtime)

The time between failure and the new leader accepting writes. Components of failover latency:

Step Typical Latency Bottleneck
Fault detection (heartbeat timeout) 5-30 seconds Conservative timeout vs. fast detection tradeoff
Leader election / consensus 1-5 seconds Raft election: ~1 RTT for election, ~1 RTT for log replication
Client rerouting (DNS/VIP update) 1-30 seconds DNS TTL (set to 5-10s for failover scenarios)
Application connection re-establishment 0.5-5 seconds Connection pool drain and reconnect
Total 10-60 seconds

4. Cascading Failover (Failover Storm)

If multiple leaders fail simultaneously (common during a rack or AZ failure), failover of each node can overload the monitoring system, election system, and remaining nodes. Controllers compete for resources, causing additional failures.

Failover Techniques

Raft Election

Used by etcd, CockroachDB, TiKV, Consul. When a follower does not receive heartbeats within the election timeout, it becomes a candidate and requests votes. A candidate needs votes from a majority to become leader. Term numbers prevent old leaders from committing writes.

Orchestrator-Based

A dedicated failover orchestrator (e.g., Orchestrator for MySQL, Patroni for PostgreSQL) monitors the cluster topology and executes failover decisions. The orchestrator runs as a separate service with its own quorum to avoid split brain in the orchestrator itself.

Orchestrator-based Failover (Patroni pattern): [Patroni-1] [Patroni-2] [Patroni-3] | | | +---- etcd/ZooKeeper quorum -----+ | [PostgreSQL Leader] | | | | health check | v | Patroni detects: Leader unhealthy | Patroni contacts etcd: "Can I promote Node-2?" | etcd (quorum): "Yes, you have the lock" | Patroni promotes Node-2: 1. Stop replication 2. pg_ctl promote 3. Update etcd with new leader | Application proxy reads etcd: "New leader is Node-2" | Traffic rerouted

etcd Locks

Use etcd (or ZooKeeper) to hold a leader lock. The healthy leader holds a lease. If the lease expires, another node can acquire the lock and become leader. This avoids split brain because etcd guarantees only one node holds the lock at a time.

Common Interview Questions

  1. "Walk me through what happens when your database leader crashes." — Detection (heartbeat timeout), election/promotion (select best follower, promote), rerouting (update DNS/proxy), recovery (old leader rejoins as follower). Discuss RPO, split brain risk, and failover duration.
  2. "How do you minimize data loss during failover?" — Semi-sync replication (at least one follower confirms receipt), promote least-lagging follower, synchronous replication for critical data. Discuss RPO requirements with the interviewer.
  3. "How does Kubernetes etcd handle leader failover?" — etcd uses Raft. When the leader fails, followers time out and start an election. The candidate with the most up-to-date log wins. Failover takes 2-3 election timeouts (typically 5-10 seconds). Kubernetes API server re-establishes its watch on the new leader.
  4. "Design a failover system for a MySQL cluster." — Orchestrator monitors replication lag and health. On failure: (1) remove old leader from proxy config, (2) select most up-to-date follower, (3) promote via SET GLOBAL read_only=0, (4) update ProxySQL routing, (5) reconfigure remaining followers. Discuss semi-sync for RPO, Orchestrator's HA trio for orchestrator reliability.

What to Skip

Avoid virtual IP (VIP) takeover details: ARP cache, gratuitous ARP, VIP failover scripts are infrastructure details. Mention the concept but skip implementation.

Avoid specific orchestrator configuration: Knowing that Orchestrator/Patroni exists is sufficient. Do not prepare YAML configurations.

Avoid multi-datacenter failover: Cross-DC failover adds significant complexity (latency, split brain, consensus across WAN). Focus on single-DC failover unless asked about multi-DC specifically.
