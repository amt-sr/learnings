3.4.3 Consistency

Difficulty: ⭐⭐⭐☆☆ Interview Frequency: ★★★★☆

Goal

  • Define ACID consistency as the preservation of database invariants and constraints
  • Understand that consistency is a shared responsibility between the database and the application
  • Clearly distinguish ACID consistency from distributed consistency (CAP theorem)
  • Explain the types of database constraints and how they enforce valid state transitions
  • Discuss invariants and why they are fundamental to correct system design

Definition

ACID Consistency guarantees that a transaction takes the database from one valid state to another. A valid state is one that satisfies all defined rules, constraints, triggers, and invariants. Unlike the other three ACID properties, consistency is partially the application's responsibility -- the database can only enforce constraints that have been explicitly declared. The correctness of business logic and domain-specific invariants is the developer's job.

Warning -- most common interview confusion: ACID Consistency is NOT the same as "read-your-writes consistency" or "strong consistency" in distributed systems (CAP theorem). ACID consistency is about maintaining integrity constraints and valid state transitions within a single database node. When someone says "our system is eventually consistent," they are talking about distributed replication consistency, NOT ACID consistency. These are completely different concepts.

Performance impact of constraints: Foreign keys are the most expensive constraint in write-heavy workloads because every INSERT/UPDATE must verify the referenced row exists in the parent table. In sharded systems, foreign keys across shards are often dropped entirely and enforced at the application level. Instagram, for example, removed all foreign keys from their sharded PostgreSQL setup for this reason.

Why ACID Consistency Matters

Without consistency enforcement, the database can enter states that violate business rules, leading to:

  • Data corruption: Orphaned rows (order referencing a deleted user), negative balances, duplicate keys
  • Application errors: Code assumes valid data but encounters impossible states, causing cascading failures
  • Financial loss: In payment systems, inventory systems, and trading platforms, violated invariants directly translate to monetary losses
  • Compliance violations: Regulatory requirements (SOC 2, PCI-DSS) often mandate data integrity controls
Key insight: ACID consistency is the property that makes databases trustworthy. Atomicity ensures all-or-nothing, durability ensures persistence, isolation ensures concurrent correctness -- but consistency ensures that the data itself is meaningful. A database can have perfect atomicity, durability, and isolation and still be useless if the data violates your business rules.

Consistency in Sharded and Distributed Databases

When you shard a database, maintaining ACID consistency across shards becomes challenging:

  • Cross-shard foreign keys: Most sharded databases (Vitess, CockroachDB) do not support foreign keys across shards. The application must enforce referential integrity.
  • Cross-shard unique constraints: Ensuring global uniqueness (e.g., username uniqueness) across shards requires coordination. Solutions include: global ID generation (Snowflake), a separate mapping table, or distributed locks.
  • Distributed transactions: Spanner and CockroachDB provide cross-shard ACID transactions, but with higher latency than single-shard operations.
Practical approach: Design your sharding key so that most operations stay within a single shard (entity-oriented sharding). This preserves ACID consistency for the majority of operations without cross-shard coordination. Cross-shard operations should be asynchronous (event-driven) where possible.

Invariants as a Design Tool

In system design, defining invariants early is one of the most powerful techniques:

SystemKey Invariants
BankingSum of all account balances = total money in system (no money created/destroyed)
InventoryItems sold <= items in stock at time of sale
VotingEach user can vote at most once per election
SchedulingNo two events can book the same resource at the same time
IdentityEach email address maps to exactly one user account
Interview tip: When asked to design a system, start by defining the invariants. For example, "In my payment system, the key invariant is that money is never created or destroyed -- every debit must have a corresponding credit." This demonstrates senior-level thinking: define correctness first, then design the system to maintain it.

Common Interview Questions

  1. What does the "C" in ACID stand for? How is it different from the "C" in CAP?
  2. What types of constraints can a database enforce? Give examples.
  3. Why is ACID consistency partially the application's responsibility?
  4. How do foreign keys affect write performance? When would you disable them?
  5. What happens to ACID consistency when you shard a database?
  6. Define three invariants for a ride-sharing system. How would you enforce them?
  7. Can you have ACID consistency without ACID isolation? Why or why not?
  8. How does a distributed transaction maintain consistency across shards?
  9. What is the difference between a check constraint and an application-level invariant?
  10. A transaction passes all database constraints but violates a business rule. Is ACID consistency maintained?

What to Skip

In an interview setting, skip:
  • CAP theorem deep-dives when discussing ACID consistency -- these are different concepts that are easily conflated
  • Detailed syntax for creating constraints in specific SQL dialects
  • Advanced constraint types like deferred constraints, partial indexes -- unless specifically asked
  • Data validation at the application layer (ORM validators, form validation) -- focus on database-level consistency
  • Distributed consistency models (causal, eventual, linearizable) -- these belong in a separate discussion about replication, not ACID consistency
