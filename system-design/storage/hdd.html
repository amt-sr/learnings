Hard Disk Drives (HDD)

  • Seek time vs rotational latency vs transfer time — and which dominates

  • How HDDs compare to SSDs and when HDDs still make sense

Definition

A Hard Disk Drive (HDD) is an electromechanical storage device that uses spinning magnetic platters and a moving read/write head to store and retrieve data. Data is written in concentric circles called tracks, which are divided into sectors. The fundamental limitation is mechanical: the head must physically move to the correct position and wait for the data to rotate underneath it.

[SecN] ↑ Read/Write Head reads one sector as platter spins Multiple platters share the same actuator arm: A "cylinder" = same track number across all platters

When the OS requests data at a logical block address (LBA), the disk controller:

  1. Seeks — moves the actuator arm to the correct track
  2. Waits — rotates the platter until the correct sector passes under the head
  3. Transfers — reads or writes the data as the sector moves past

Access Time Breakdown

ComponentDescriptionTypical Time
Seek TimeMove the head to the correct track (mechanical arm movement)3–10 ms
Rotational LatencyWait for the correct sector to spin under the head (avg = half rotation)2–4 ms (7200 RPM), 2 ms (15K RPM)
Transfer TimeRead/write data as it passes under the head~0.01–0.1 ms
Total Random AccessSum of all three~5–10 ms (~100–200 IOPS)
Key insight: Seek and rotational latency dominate the access time. Transfer time is negligible. This is why a single 1 MB sequential read is almost as fast as a single 4 KB random read — the positioning cost is paid once regardless of transfer size. But 1000 random 4 KB reads each pay the full ~5–10 ms penalty, yielding catastrophic throughput.

Why Sequential is Fast (Interview Angle)

After the initial seek, the head stays in position while data streams under it at the platter's rotational speed. At 7200 RPM, a track on the outer edge passes under the head at roughly 50–100 MB/s. The disk firmware and OS also leverage read-ahead: when it detects a sequential pattern, the OS prefetches upcoming blocks into the page cache, further hiding latency.

Interview tip: When asked "how many IOPS does an HDD handle?", the answer depends on RPM. A 7200 RPM drive gives ~75–100 random IOPS. This is why the buffer pool is critical — at 100 IOPS, you can only do 100 random disk reads per second. Everything else must come from memory.

When HDDs Still Make Sense

  • Cold storage and archival: Data accessed infrequently does not need SSD speed. Amazon S3 Glacier and similar services use HDD-based storage behind the scenes.
  • Backups: Sequential write performance on HDD is adequate for backup workloads, and the cost per GB is significantly lower.
  • Large-scale data lakes: When you have petabytes of data (e.g., Hadoop HDFS clusters), the cost difference between HDD and SSD storage is enormous.
  • Media streaming and content delivery: Sequential reads of large files (video, audio) work well with HDD throughput.
  • Write-heavy append-only logs: WAL-style sequential writes on HDD can reach 100+ MB/s, which is often sufficient.
Design takeaway: Even though most production databases now use SSDs, understanding HDD characteristics matters because many storage engine design decisions (B-tree structure, LSM-tree compaction, WAL layout) were optimized for HDD behavior. Understanding why these designs exist makes you a stronger system designer.

Common Interview Questions

  • "Why are random reads so slow on HDDs?"
    Each random read requires the mechanical actuator arm to move to the correct track (seek time, 3–10 ms) and then wait for the platter to rotate the target sector under the head (rotational latency, 2–4 ms). This mechanical overhead is ~10,000x slower than electronic RAM access. Compare to SSDs where there is no mechanical movement — the controller addresses flash cells electronically.
  • "How many IOPS can a typical HDD handle?"
    A 7200 RPM drive achieves approximately 75–100 IOPS for random 4 KB reads. A 15K RPM enterprise drive reaches 180–210 IOPS. At 100 IOPS, processing 10,000 random reads takes 100 seconds. This is why databases heavily cache in buffer pools and why LSM-trees convert random writes into sequential appends.
  • "Why does the WAL perform well on HDDs?"
    The WAL is append-only, meaning writes go to the next sequential position on disk. The head rarely needs to seek — it just waits for the current sector to arrive. Sequential writes on HDD can reach 100–200 MB/s, comparable to SSDs. This is the core insight that makes write-ahead logging viable even on spinning disks.
  • "When would you still choose HDDs over SSDs?"
    Cost-sensitive scenarios with large data volumes and predominantly sequential access patterns: backups, archival storage, data lakes, media serving. An 8 TB HDD costs ~$160 ($0.02/GB) while an 8 TB SSD costs ~$800+ ($0.10/GB). For 100 TB of cold data, HDDs save tens of thousands of dollars.
